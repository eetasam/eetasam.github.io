OVERVIEW
Case Study: Whoosh Mail is a webapp used to manage, send, and track email campaigns.
AUDIENCE
This app is built for small businesses owners and employees across the US.
Process Deck
PROBLEM
A Two-Flow Web App
Whoosh Mail is looking to develop an email marketing campaign web app with two distinct flows for Admin and Basic users.
INITIAL RESEARCH
After interviewing grocery store customers, a few pain points were revealed:
Three main issues were apparent in the grocery store experience:
  1. Interviewees were frustrated that they cannot find every item they need in one store, but they are willing to shop around if they have to.
  2. Interviewees were frustrated by long lines in and outside of the store, as well as difficult checkout procedures in stores.
  3. Interviewees have a tough time keeping track of their shopping list as they traverse the aisles, and waste time trying to find the items they need.
PROJECT TARGETS
My goal was to develop an app for a local grocery store. that allows customers to easily browse deals and provides rewards for frequent shoppers. It could incorporate an in-store shopping list, sorted by aisles, so that customers could optimize their shopping experiences by reducing time spent navigating the store.
1. In Store
Users are gaining no useful information from the current banner. I moved to replace the existing banner with a smaller image carousel that cycles through the featured T-shirt of the month as well as the charity of the month to meet the stakeholder’s requests.
2. Create a navigational element
In order to make the site more easily navigable, I introduced a simple navigation bar in the header that organizes the product offerings by type, rather than presenting all offerings on the landing page.
3. Update color scheme and branding
The current color palette is off-putting. I changed the use of colors on the site, and reserved bright colors for call-to-actions and points of interest to reduce the loudness of the pages. I also changed the color of the text (as well as maintain consistent typography) in the body, so that the text is more readable. These changes, paired with an updated logo, effectively gave the site a more "campsite" vibe, as presented in the project mood board.
LOW-FIDELITY WIREFRAMES
I built wireframes for both desktop and mobile versions of the site.
DESIGN DECISIONS
After reviewing my wireframes, I moved to create high-fidelity mockups. I stuck as close to the wireframe design as possible, except on the product page. I condensed the elements on this page to reduce clutter.
LEARNINGS
Typography
I experimented with many different fonts when I was configuring my mockups, and each one gave my website a different look and feel. It’s amazing how a seemingly small change like rounded letter corners can completely change how something appeals visually.
Prototyping in Photoshop
I learned how to properly implement vector masking in Photoshop. Illustrator is more commonly used for this type of work, but I enjoyed finding the work-arounds for vector asks in Photoshop.
The Value of Research
Over the course of this project, I redesigned my navigation bar a total of 4 times. Each time, it felt like my choices were dated, and not a big enough improvement from the original site. I wasted a lot of time “trying things out” only to completely change my ideas. If I could start over, I would spend more time researching and benchmarking contemporary designs for ecommerce websites, so I could take sincere inspiration from them.
